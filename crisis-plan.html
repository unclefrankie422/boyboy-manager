事情发生到现在已经过去一个月了。在这一个月里，我们没有大范围对外公布这个消息，因为我们首先需要做的是梳理客户名单、评估影响范围、制定一个相对完整的应对方案，然后才能有底气地去面对客户。今天趁您过来的机会，我想把目前的情况、我们做的努力、以及接下来需要您支持的几个关键决策，完整地向您汇报。

第二部分：当前最大的挑战——人手严重不足

在讲具体策略之前，我必须先把我们面临的最大困难跟您说清楚，那就是人手问题。这个问题不是某个人的能力问题，而是整个团队在总量上与客户规模不匹配的问题。我们现在的团队配置是：一位外国总经理、一位顾问也就是我自己、一位华南地区的同事、以及一位在华东的同事。而我们要面对的是XX留下的超过50%的客户。这个差距是实实在在的。

我先说华南地区。XX走后，华南只剩下一位同事。这位同事在公司的时间相对较短，他的技术能力和产品知识非常扎实，这一点是毋庸置疑的，在日常的技术对接和后台支持方面他做得很好。但XX之前承担的角色远远超出了普通销售的范畴——他是客户的朋友，是信任的载体，是20年关系的维护者。这种角色跟一个人的年龄、阅历、沟通方式都有密切关系。我们这位年轻同事在技术层面完全可以胜任，但在客户关系的维护和深度沟通方面，他还需要一个成长的过程。这不是他的错，而是角色转换本身就需要时间。

再说华东地区。我们还有一位同事在华东，他的能力是够的，但他人在华东，不可能长期跑华南。上周我们专门去了一趟华东，发现XX在华东也有一些客户，那边的客户同样面临关系接续的问题。但让华东同事两边跑是不现实的，他自己手头也有不少客户要维护。

最后说说我自己。这一个月以来，我一直在积极配合，陪同华南同事跑客户、整理名单、梳理方案。但我必须坦诚地说，我是顾问身份，不是公司的正式员工，每周能投入的时间大概也就一到两次。以这样的频率去面对超过一半的客户，是远远不够的。所以我想让您清楚地了解到：我们现在的人力状况确实存在很大的缺口，这个缺口是客观存在的，不是靠加班或者努力就能完全弥补的。我们所有的策略，都是在"人力有限"这个前提下设计的，目标是在有限的人力下做到最大程度的客户留存。

第三部分：我们已经采取的行动

虽然人手紧张，但在您来之前我们没有坐着等，已经尽力做了几件事，在这里简单向您汇报一下。

第一件事，我们花了大量时间梳理客户名单。我们把所有客户按照重要程度分成了三个级别。A档是命脉客户，数量不多，大概三到五家，但每一家都是我们最核心的生意伙伴，这些客户如果丢了，对公司的影响是非常大的，所以绝对不能丢。B档是重要客户，有十几二十家，他们有稳定的采购需求，虽然单家体量不如A档，但加在一起也是不小的一部分。C档是长尾客户，数量比较多但单家采购量不大。做完这个分类之后，我们至少清楚地知道了接下来有限的精力应该优先花在哪些客户身上。

第二件事，上周我和华南同事专程去了一趟华东。因为XX在华东也有一些客户，我们需要亲自去了解那边的状况。跑下来之后发现，华东的情况跟华南类似，那边的客户同样很依赖XX的个人关系。这次华东之行让我更加确认了一个判断：这个问题不是华南独有的，是整个公司层面的，需要从整体上来应对。

第三件事，这一个月以来，我一直陪同华南的同事去跑客户。虽然我每周只能去一到两次，覆盖不了全部客户，但至少我们已经在行动了。我们拜访了几家重点客户，了解了他们的反应和态度，也初步试探了他们对继续合作的意愿。这些一线的反馈，对我们制定接下来的策略是非常重要的参考。

第四部分：稳住客户的策略与步骤

接下来我想详细讲一下我们稳住客户的策略。这个策略的核心思路是：我们不是简单地换一个人去跟客户讲产品——那样是行不通的，因为客户认的不是产品，是人。我们要做的是用公司层面的诚意来延续这份合作关系，让每一个客户感受到：XX虽然不在了，但公司对他的重视没有变，服务没有变，承诺也没有变。

具体来说，我们的策略分为四个步骤。第一步是客户分级管理，把有限的精力用在刀刃上。对于A档客户，也就是最核心的三到五家，我建议由总经理您亲自出面，我陪同翻译，一家一家登门拜访。您亲自去这个动作本身，就是在向客户传递一个最强烈的信号：公司最高层对这个客户非常重视。对于B档客户，由我陪同华南同事一起拜访，我来主导沟通，华南同事负责技术支持和日常对接。对于C档客户，先由华南同事通过电话和微信通知，告知客户服务不变、只是联系人变更，后续再由他独立跟进。这样分级处理的好处是，我们能把最稀缺的资源——也就是您的时间和我的时间——集中投放在最关键的客户身上。

第二步是制定明确的拜访节奏，做到先紧后松。第一周是黄金窗口期，我们必须在这一周内完成所有A档客户的拜访，同时尽可能多地覆盖B档客户。时间不等人，客户那边一旦有风吹草动，竞争对手随时可能趁虚而入，所以第一周的密度非常重要。第二到第四周是巩固期，A档客户由我每周跟进一次，可以通过电话或微信保持联系；B档客户每两周拜访一次；C档客户每周打一个电话。从第二个月到第三个月，我们逐步进入常态化：A档客户我每月拜访一次，加上日常的微信维护；B档客户由华南同事独立拜访，我负责抽查和把关；C档客户完全由华南同事独立负责。整个节奏的设计逻辑是：前期密集出击，先把关系接上，让客户安心；然后慢慢过渡到正常状态，不让我们自己一直处于高压运转。

第三步，也是我认为最关键的一步，就是总经理亲自参与。我必须强调这一点，因为这对稳住局势的作用是最大的。虽然您不懂中文，在沟通上需要我来翻译，但您亲自到场这个动作本身就胜过千言万语。客户看到公司最高层从西班牙飞过来，亲自站在他们面前，这个信号意味着：公司是认真的，公司对中国市场是长期投入的，XX的离开不会动摇公司的根基。具体操作上，由我来负责翻译和引导话题，您来表态和传递信心。我建议在第一周内就把A档客户全部拜访完，趁热打铁。

第四步是客户沟通的核心原则。跟客户沟通的时候，我们有三个基本原则：不隐瞒、重感情、给保障。不隐瞒就是主动告知客户XX离世的消息，但方式要得体，不能让客户觉得我们在遮掩。重感情就是让客户感受到我们珍惜这份多年的合作关系，同时也可以适当地表达XX本人的意愿——他生前一直希望公司能把服务做好、把客户照顾好。给保障就是明确告诉客户：产品供应不变、质量不变、价格不变、服务不变。至于具体怎么跟每个客户说，我们有经验，会根据每个客户的性格和情况灵活应对，这一点请您放心。

第五部分：需要您决策的事项

汇报的最后，我想提出四个需要您拍板决策的事项。这四个事项直接关系到我们能不能把前面的策略落地执行。

第一个决策是立即启动招聘。这是我们当前最紧迫的需求。我建议公司马上开始招聘一到两名销售人员，要求有模具或零配件行业的经验，同时沟通能力要强，能够独立跟客户建立信任关系。XX的离开给我们上了最深的一课：我们绝对不能再让一个人掌握超过一半的客户，这种风险是不可接受的。以后任何一个人的离职，都不能影响超过30%的业务。新人入职之后，我建议让他先跟着我跑两到三个月，从C档客户开始练手，慢慢过渡到B档，最后再接触A档。在这个过渡期内，我会把我的经验和方法都教给他，让他尽快成长起来。

第二个决策是关于我的角色定位。在过渡期的三到六个月内，我愿意增加拜访客户的频率，尽我最大的努力帮助公司稳住局面。但这里有一个问题需要您给我一个明确的说法：我是顾问，不是正式员工，客户在拜访的时候一定会问"以后谁来负责我们的业务？"我需要能给客户一个明确的答案。另外，如果我的工作量明显增加——从每周一两次变成每周三四次甚至更多——那么顾问费用是否需要做相应的调整，也希望您能考虑。

第三个决策是关于您的时间安排。前面我说了，您亲自出面拜访A档客户是我们整个策略中最关键的一环。所以我想请示您：能不能在这次来访期间，安排出时间跟我一起去拜访这几家最重要的客户？具体的行程我来安排，您只需要到场和表态。这个投入对您来说可能只是几天时间，但对稳住我们最核心的客户来说，价值是不可估量的。

第四个决策是关于预算。要把上面的策略执行下去，我们需要一笔预算来支撑。主要包括几个部分：招聘的费用、拜访客户的差旅费用——因为我们需要在华南和华东两边跑、以及一些必要的客户维护费用。这笔投入是必须的，因为它直接关系到我们能不能保住超过一半的业绩。如果为了省这点预算而丢了客户，那损失会大得多。

最后我总结一下。短期来看，我和总经理一起顶上，用公司层面的诚意来稳住最重要的VIP客户，同时在第一周内完成关键拜访。中期来看，立即招人、培养新人、逐步完成交接。长期来看，我们要建立客户分散管理机制和客户管理系统，绝不让一个人的离开再动摇公司半个业务版图。我们在这一个月里一直在努力，但人力缺口是客观存在的，单靠我们现有的团队很难完全覆盖。所以我们需要您的支持——在招人、预算、以及您个人的参与上。以上就是我的汇报，谢谢您。

Part 1: Incident Review

Thank you for making the trip from Spain. Today I would like to give you a complete report on the situation following the sudden loss of our key South China colleague, XX, including the actions we have taken and the plan we are proposing going forward.

This was a devastating blow to our entire team. XX had been with the company for 20 years. It is no exaggeration to say that the entire South China client base — from zero to where it is today — was built by him, one client at a time, over two decades. He managed more than half of our total clients, and his sales accounted for over 50% of our revenue. He knew the products inside and out, but more importantly, he had built a level of trust with clients that goes far beyond business. That kind of trust is not something you can build in a few visits. It is the result of 20 years of consistent relationship-building.

It has now been one month since the incident. During this month, we did not make a wide public announcement, because we first needed to organize our client list, assess the scope of impact, and develop a comprehensive response plan before we could face clients with confidence. Now that you are here, I want to give you a full picture: where we stand, what we have done, and the key decisions we need from you.

Part 2: Our Biggest Challenge — A Serious Staffing Shortfall

Before I get into the strategy, I need to be upfront about the biggest difficulty we face: our staffing situation. This is not a question of any individual's capability. It is a question of the overall team size being mismatched with the volume of clients we need to cover. Our current team consists of: one foreign General Manager, one consultant — which is me, one colleague in South China, and one colleague in East China. And we are facing a client base that represents more than 50% of the company's business. That gap is real.

Let me start with South China. After XX's passing, only one colleague remains in South China. He has been with the company for a relatively shorter time. His technical knowledge and product expertise are very solid — that is without question, and he does excellent work in technical support and backend coordination. But the role XX played went far beyond that of a typical salesperson. He was a trusted friend to clients. He was the carrier of 20 years of relationships. That kind of role is tied to a person's age, experience, and communication style. Our young colleague is fully capable on the technical side, but when it comes to maintaining deep client relationships and handling sensitive communications, he needs time to grow into the role. This is not his fault — it is simply the nature of the transition.

Now let me address East China. We have one colleague based in East China. He is capable, but he is in East China — he cannot regularly cover South China. Last week we made a special trip to East China and found that XX also had clients there. Those clients face the same relationship continuity challenge. But asking the East China colleague to constantly travel between regions is not realistic — he has his own clients to manage.

Finally, regarding myself. Over the past month, I have been actively involved — accompanying our South China colleague on client visits, organizing the client list, and developing plans. But I must be honest: I am a consultant, not a full-time employee. I can realistically commit to one or two visits per week. At that frequency, trying to cover more than half the client base is far from sufficient. So I want you to understand clearly: our current staffing has a significant gap. This gap is objective — it cannot be fully closed by working harder or putting in longer hours. Every strategy I am about to present is designed with this constraint in mind. Our goal is to maximize client retention with the limited resources we have.

Part 3: Actions Already Taken

Despite the staffing constraints, we have not been sitting idle. Before your arrival, we did everything we could with the resources available to us. Let me briefly report on three things we have done.

The first thing we did was spend considerable time organizing our client list. We categorized all clients into three tiers based on their importance to the business. Tier A consists of our critical accounts — only three to five of them, but each one is an essential business partner. Losing any of these would have a major impact on the company, so they absolutely cannot be lost. Tier B consists of important clients — about ten to twenty — who have stable purchasing needs. While each individual account may not be as large as Tier A, together they represent a significant portion of our business. Tier C consists of long-tail clients — larger in number but with smaller individual purchasing volumes. After completing this categorization, we at least had a clear picture of where our limited energy should be prioritized.

The second thing we did was travel to East China last week with our South China colleague. XX had clients in East China as well, and we needed to see the situation there firsthand. What we found confirmed our concern — clients in East China are equally dependent on XX's personal relationships. This trip reinforced a key insight: this is not a South China problem alone. The third thing we did was begin client visits. Over the past month, I have been accompanying our South China colleague to visit clients. Although I can only manage one or two visits per week — which is nowhere near enough to cover the full client base — at least we have been in motion. Part 4: Client Retention Strategy and Action Steps

Now let me walk you through our retention strategy in detail. The core philosophy is this: we are not simply replacing one person with another to talk about products. That approach would not work, because clients chose us for the person, not just the product. What we need to do is use company-level sincerity to maintain these partnerships. We need every client to feel that while XX is gone, the company's commitment to them has not changed, the service has not changed, and our promises have not changed.

Specifically, our strategy consists of four steps. The first step is tiered client management — focusing our limited energy where it matters most. For Tier A clients — the three to five most critical accounts — I recommend that you, the General Manager, visit each one personally, with me accompanying as interpreter. The act of you going in person sends the strongest possible signal: the highest level of the company values this client deeply. For Tier B clients, I will visit together with our South China colleague. I will lead the communication, while he handles technical support and day-to-day coordination. For Tier C clients, our South China colleague will first notify them by phone or WeChat, informing them that service continues and only the contact person has changed, then follow up independently. The benefit of this tiered approach is that we can concentrate our scarcest resources — your time and mine — on the most critical clients.

The second step is establishing a clear visit cadence — intensive upfront, then gradually normalizing. Week 1 is our golden window. We must complete all Tier A client visits within this week, while covering as many Tier B clients as possible. Time is not on our side — if there is any sign of instability, competitors may move in quickly. So the density of the first week is critical. Weeks 2 through 4 are the consolidation phase: Tier A clients receive weekly follow-up from me via phone or WeChat; Tier B clients are visited every two weeks; Tier C clients receive a weekly phone call. From month 2 to month 3, we gradually transition to normalization: Tier A clients get a monthly visit from me plus daily WeChat contact; Tier B clients are visited independently by our South China colleague, with me doing spot-checks and oversight; Tier C clients are fully managed by him. The logic behind this cadence is simple: start with intensive engagement to re-establish the connection and give clients confidence, then gradually transition to a sustainable routine so we are not running at maximum intensity indefinitely.

The third step — and I believe this is the single most important step — is your personal involvement. I want to emphasize this because it will have the greatest impact on stabilizing the situation. Even though you do not speak Chinese and will need me to translate, your physical presence at these meetings speaks louder than any words I could say. When a client sees that the General Manager has flown from Spain to stand in front of them, the message is unmistakable: the company is serious, the company is committed to the China market for the long term, and XX's passing does not shake the company's foundation. In practice, I will handle translation and guide the conversation, while you provide the reassurance and demonstrate commitment. I recommend we complete all Tier A visits within the first week, while the momentum is fresh.

The fourth step is about the core principles for client communication. When we engage with clients, we follow three basic principles: transparency, sincerity, and assurance. Transparency means we proactively inform clients about XX's passing, but in a tactful way — we do not want clients to feel we are hiding anything. Sincerity means we let clients feel that we genuinely value these years of partnership, and we can also appropriately express XX's own wishes — he always wanted the company to continue delivering great service and taking care of clients. Assurance means we explicitly tell clients: product supply remains unchanged, quality remains unchanged, pricing remains unchanged, and service remains unchanged. As for the specific way we communicate with each client, we have experience and will adapt flexibly based on each client's personality and situation. You can trust us on this.
